Evolution and Making of the Indian Constitution

1. Introduction

The Indian Constitution is the supreme law of India, drafted to establish the country's fundamental political principles, framework, and government procedures.

It embodies the ideals of justice, liberty, equality, and fraternity, reflecting the aspirations of the Indian people.

2. Colonial Legacy

The Government of India Act 1935 was a significant precursor that laid down a large part of the administrative structure and principles, such as federalism, that were later adopted in the Indian Constitution.

3. The Constituent Assembly

The Constituent Assembly was established under the Cabinet Mission Plan of 1946. The Assembly consisted of members elected from the provinces and princely states.

It had 299 members, including prominent leaders like Dr. B.R. Ambedkar, Jawaharlal Nehru, and Sardar Patel, who played key roles in drafting the Constitution.

4. Drafting Process

The Constituent Assembly met for the first time on December 9, 1946, and held 11 sessions over a period of almost three years, with the final session concluding on November 26, 1949.

The Drafting Committee, chaired by Dr. B.R. Ambedkar, was responsible for drafting the Constitution. The first draft was presented to the Assembly in February 1948.

5. Key Features of the Indian Constitution

The Indian Constitution is the longest written constitution in the world, containing 395 articles, 12 schedules, and numerous amendments.

The Preamble reflects the vision and goals of the Constitution, stating India as a "Sovereign Socialist Secular Democratic Republic."

It guarantees Fundamental Rights and Duties to all citizens, alongside the Directive Principles of State Policy, which were inspired by the Irish Constitution.

The Constitution provides a federal structure with a strong center, although India is a federal state.

It can be amended, allowing it to evolve over time, with provisions to protect its basic structure.

6. Adoption and Enforcement

The Constitution was adopted on November 26, 1949, by the Constituent Assembly.

It came into effect on January 26, 1950, a date chosen to commemorate the declaration of Purna Swaraj (complete independence) in 1930.

7. Impact and Legacy

The Constitution established India as a democratic republic with a parliamentary system of government.

It provided provisions for the upliftment of socially and economically backward sections of society, including reservations in education and employment.

The Constitution maintained legal continuity with British law while introducing new laws to reflect India's aspirations as an independent nation.


Meaning and Significance of the Indian Constitution

1. Meaning of the Indian Constitution

  • Fundamental Law: The Indian Constitution is the supreme legal document that outlines the structure, powers, and functions of the government institutions and sets out the fundamental rights and duties of citizens.
  • Framework for Governance: It establishes the framework for the governance of India, defining the distribution of powers between the central government and the states, as well as the division of powers among the legislative, executive, and judicial branches.
  • Guiding Document: The Constitution serves as the guiding document for all laws and policies in the country, ensuring that they are aligned with the principles of democracy, justice, and equality.

2. Significance of the Indian Constitution

  • Establishment of a Democratic Republic:
    • The Constitution declares India as a "Sovereign Socialist Secular Democratic Republic," meaning the country is independent, committed to social justice, secularism, and governed by democratic principles.
    • It provides for a parliamentary system of government where the people elect their representatives.
  • Protection of Fundamental Rights:
    • The Constitution guarantees fundamental rights to all citizens, including the right to equality, freedom, protection against exploitation, freedom of religion, cultural and educational rights, and the right to constitutional remedies.
    • These rights are essential for the protection of individual liberties and maintaining social justice.
  • Social and Economic Justice:
    • Through the Directive Principles of State Policy, the Constitution aims to create a welfare state by guiding the government to work towards the economic and social well-being of the people.
    • These principles include promoting social justice, equitable distribution of resources, and the elimination of inequalities.
  • Federalism with a Strong Center:
    • The Constitution establishes a federal structure with a clear division of powers between the central government and state governments.
    • However, it also provides the central government with significant powers to maintain the unity and integrity of the nation.
  • Secularism:
    • The Constitution ensures that India is a secular state, where all religions are respected, and the state does not favor any religion.
    • This principle promotes religious harmony and protects the rights of religious minorities.
  • Amendability:
    • The Constitution is a living document that can be amended to reflect the changing needs and aspirations of the society.
    • This flexibility ensures that the Constitution remains relevant and adaptable over time.
  • Establishment of Rule of Law:
    • The Constitution establishes the rule of law, meaning that all individuals and institutions, including the government, are subject to the law.
    • This principle ensures accountability and prevents the misuse of power.
  • Unity and Integrity:
    • By defining India as a single nation with a common citizenship, the Constitution promotes national unity and integrity while respecting the diversity of the country.
    • Provisions like a single judiciary, uniform civil code (in spirit), and a common electoral system contribute to the cohesion of the nation.
  • Guidance for Governance:
    • The Constitution provides a comprehensive framework for governance, detailing the powers and functions of the President, Prime Minister, Parliament, State Legislatures, and other institutions.
    • It ensures that these institutions operate within the boundaries set by the Constitution, maintaining a system of checks and balances.
  • Empowerment of the People:
    • The Constitution empowers the people by granting them the right to vote, ensuring their participation in the democratic process.
    • It also empowers citizens to approach the courts if their rights are violated, ensuring justice and accountability.

    Composition of the Indian Constitution

    The Indian Constitution is one of the most detailed and comprehensive legal documents in the world. It lays down the fundamental framework for governance and reflects the aspirations of the Indian people. Below is a detailed overview of its composition:

    1. Preamble

    Introduction to the Constitution: The Preamble serves as the introduction to the Constitution, outlining the objectives and guiding principles of the document.

    Key Elements:

    • Sovereign: India is independent and free from external control.
    • Socialist: Commitment to social and economic equality.
    • Secular: Equal respect for all religions.
    • Democratic: Power rests with the people who elect their representatives.
    • Republic: The head of state is elected, not a hereditary monarch.
    • Justice, Liberty, Equality, Fraternity: Core values that the Constitution seeks to promote.

    2. Parts and Articles

    Structure: The Constitution is divided into 25 parts, each dealing with specific aspects of governance and law.

    Key Parts:

    • Part I: The Union and Its Territory: Defines the country and its territorial integrity.
    • Part II: Citizenship: Provisions related to citizenship at the commencement of the Constitution.
    • Part III: Fundamental Rights: Guarantees civil liberties and rights such as equality, freedom of speech, and religious freedom.
    • Part IV: Directive Principles of State Policy: Guidelines for the state to create policies aimed at social and economic welfare.
    • Part IVA: Fundamental Duties: Lists the duties of citizens toward the nation.
    • Part V: The Union: Outlines the powers, functions, and composition of the Union government, including the President, Vice-President, Parliament, and Council of Ministers.
    • Part VI: The States: Details the structure and functioning of state governments.
    • Part IX: Panchayats: Provisions for the decentralization of power through local self-government (Panchayati Raj).
    • Part IXA: Municipalities: Provisions for urban local bodies.
    • Part X: The Scheduled and Tribal Areas: Special provisions for the administration of scheduled and tribal areas.
    • Part XI: Relations Between the Union and the States: Division of powers between the central and state governments.
    • Part XIII: Trade and Commerce within the Territory of India: Provisions for free trade and commerce across the country.
    • Part XX: Amendment of the Constitution: Procedure for amending the Constitution.

    3. Schedules

    Supplementary Provisions: The Constitution includes 12 Schedules, which provide additional details that support the articles in the Constitution.

    Key Schedules:

    • Schedule I: Lists the states and union territories along with their territorial jurisdictions.
    • Schedule II: Specifies the emoluments for officials like the President, Governors, Judges, etc.
    • Schedule III: Contains forms of oaths and affirmations for various constitutional posts.
    • Schedule V: Provisions for the administration of scheduled areas and tribes.
    • Schedule VII: Divides powers between the Union and State governments into three lists—Union List, State List, and Concurrent List.
    • Schedule IX: Lists laws that are exempted from judicial review (though this has been subject to judicial scrutiny).
    • Schedule X: Contains provisions related to disqualification of MPs and MLAs on grounds of defection (anti-defection law).
    • Schedule XI: Details the powers, authority, and responsibilities of Panchayats.
    • Schedule XII: Details the powers, authority, and responsibilities of Municipalities.

    4. Amendments

    Dynamic Document: The Constitution provides for amendments to adapt to changing needs. Article 368 details the amendment process.

    Number of Amendments: As of now, there have been over 100 amendments, reflecting the Constitution's flexibility and responsiveness to new challenges.

    5. Basic Structure Doctrine

    Judicial Safeguard: The Supreme Court introduced the Basic Structure Doctrine in the landmark Kesavananda Bharati case (1973), stating that while the Constitution can be amended, its basic structure cannot be altered. This includes fundamental principles like the supremacy of the Constitution, the rule of law, and the separation of powers.

    4. Language

    Multilingual Document: The original Constitution was written in both English and Hindi, ensuring accessibility to the diverse population of India.

Preamble of the Indian Constitution

1. Text of the Preamble

"We, the people of India, having solemnly resolved to constitute India into a Sovereign Socialist Secular Democratic Republic and to secure to all its citizens:

  • Justice, social, economic, and political;
  • Liberty of thought, expression, belief, faith, and worship;
  • Equality of status and of opportunity;
  • And to promote among them all Fraternity assuring the dignity of the individual and the unity and integrity of the Nation;

In our Constituent Assembly this twenty-sixth day of November 1949, do hereby adopt, enact, and give to ourselves this Constitution."

2. Components of the Preamble

  • Sovereign: India is free and independent, not subject to external authority.
  • Socialist: India's commitment to eliminate inequality and provide social and economic justice. This word was added in the 42nd Amendment (1976).
  • Secular: The state has no official religion and treats all religions equally. Added by the 42nd Amendment (1976).
  • Democratic: Power rests with the people, who elect representatives at all levels of government.
  • Republic: The head of state, i.e., the President, is elected rather than a hereditary monarch.

3. Objectives Outlined in the Preamble

  • Justice: Aimed at providing social, economic, and political fairness to all citizens, eliminating inequalities.
  • Liberty: Ensures freedom of thought, expression, belief, faith, and worship, essential in a democratic society.
  • Equality: Provides equal opportunities to all, regardless of background, religion, or social class, aiming to eliminate discrimination.
  • Fraternity: Promotes a sense of brotherhood among all citizens, fostering unity and ensuring the dignity of the individual and national integrity.

Importance of the Preamble

1. Reflection of the Constitution's Spirit

  • The Preamble encapsulates the essence of the Indian Constitution, reflecting the goals, aspirations, and values that it seeks to achieve.
  • It highlights the vision of the framers of the Constitution and their commitment to building an inclusive, just, and progressive society.

2. Source of Authority

  • The phrase "We, the people of India" establishes that the Constitution derives its authority from the citizens of India.
  • It symbolizes that the Constitution is a document created by the people and for the people.

3. Guiding Principles for Governance

  • The Preamble sets forth the philosophical foundations of the Constitution, serving as a guide for interpreting and implementing the various provisions of the Constitution.
  • The values of justice, liberty, equality, and fraternity direct the government's policies and actions towards the overall well-being of citizens.

4. Basis for Judicial Interpretation

  • The Preamble has been used by the courts, especially the Supreme Court, to understand the true spirit and intent behind the provisions of the Constitution.
  • It has played a crucial role in several landmark judgments, including the Kesavananda Bharati case (1973), where the Supreme Court upheld the importance of the Preamble and introduced the basic structure doctrine.

5. Inspiration for Lawmaking

  • The ideals enshrined in the Preamble inspire lawmakers to create laws that promote justice, liberty, equality, and fraternity.
  • It helps in shaping policies that are in line with the broader constitutional vision of a welfare state.

6. Symbol of National Unity

  • By promoting fraternity and emphasizing the unity and integrity of the nation, the Preamble acts as a binding force that holds together the diverse cultures, languages, and religions of India.

7. Dynamic and Progressive Nature

  • The Preamble allows the Constitution to evolve with time by providing broad, flexible principles that can be interpreted to meet changing social and political circumstances.
  • Amendments like the addition of "Socialist" and "Secular" through the 42nd Amendment demonstrate the evolving nature of India's constitutional vision.

Aims, Objectives, and Scope of the Preamble

1. Aims of the Preamble

  • Establish a Sovereign State:
    • Aim: To create an independent nation, free from external control or interference.
    • India, through its Constitution, asserts its complete sovereignty and independence in making decisions related to internal and external affairs.
  • Promote Socialism:
    • Aim: To reduce inequality in income, status, and standard of living, ensuring equitable distribution of wealth and resources.
    • The concept of socialism embedded in the Preamble emphasizes the government’s role in reducing disparities through various social and economic policies.
  • Ensure Secularism:
    • Aim: To maintain a state that respects all religions equally, without endorsing or favoring any particular religion.
    • The secular nature of the Constitution ensures that religious freedom is protected and that the state remains neutral in religious matters.
  • Strengthen Democracy:
    • Aim: To establish a government of the people, by the people, and for the people, where citizens have the right to elect their representatives.
    • The democratic framework ensures active participation of citizens in the governance process, with the government being accountable to the people.
  • Establish a Republic:
    • Aim: To ensure that the head of state is elected and not a hereditary monarch.
    • This guarantees that the highest office in the country is accessible to all citizens, regardless of their background or lineage.

2. Objectives of the Preamble

  • Justice:
    • Social Justice: Aim to create a society based on equality and fairness, where opportunities are not determined by caste, creed, or religion.
    • Economic Justice: Ensuring that wealth and resources are distributed equitably, and that the government provides for the economic needs of all citizens.
    • Political Justice: Guaranteeing that all citizens have an equal right to participate in the political process, with free and fair elections.
  • Liberty:
    • The Preamble aims to secure the liberty of thought, expression, belief, faith, and worship.
    • This ensures that individuals have the freedom to hold and express their views without fear of repression.
  • Equality:
    • The objective of equality aims to eliminate discrimination based on religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth.
    • It ensures equal treatment before the law and equal opportunities in all spheres of life.
  • Fraternity:
    • The Preamble promotes fraternity, which assures the dignity of the individual and the unity and integrity of the nation.
    • This objective fosters a sense of brotherhood and national unity among the diverse population of India.

3. Scope of the Preamble

  • Interpretative Tool:
    • The Preamble serves as an interpretative tool for understanding the provisions of the Constitution.
    • Courts often refer to the Preamble to determine the true spirit and intent of the law.
  • Guiding Framework:
    • The Preamble lays down the guiding principles for the governance of the country.
    • It sets the broad objectives that the Constitution seeks to achieve, thereby influencing the formulation of laws and policies.
  • Foundation of the Constitution:
    • The Preamble acts as the foundation of the Constitution, summarizing its core values and goals. It provides a concise statement of the purpose and philosophy behind the Constitution.
    • It outlines the ideological foundation upon which the entire structure of the Constitution is built, emphasizing the importance of justice, liberty, equality, and fraternity.
    Reflects the Aspirations of the People:
    • The Preamble reflects the aspirations of the Indian people. It embodies the hopes and dreams of the citizens for a just and equitable society.
    • It ensures that the Constitution remains relevant to the changing needs and aspirations of the society by encapsulating the core values that are timeless.
    Adaptability and Flexibility:
    • While the Preamble sets out the fundamental objectives of the Constitution, its broad and general language allows it to adapt to changing circumstances.
    • This flexibility ensures that the Preamble remains relevant even as society evolves, enabling the Constitution to respond to new challenges and needs.

    Salient Features of the Indian Constitution

    The Indian Constitution, adopted on November 26, 1949, and effective from January 26, 1950, is a comprehensive and dynamic document that outlines the framework for governance in India. Here are the key salient features that distinguish the Indian Constitution:

    1. Written and Detailed Constitution

    • Comprehensive Document: The Indian Constitution is one of the lengthiest written constitutions in the world, containing 470 articles, 25 parts, and 12 schedules (as of now).
    • Exhaustive Coverage: It includes provisions not only for the structure of government but also for the rights of citizens, the powers and functions of various organs of government, and the relationship between the Union and the States.

    2. Blend of Rigidity and Flexibility

    • Amendment Process: The Constitution provides a balance between rigidity and flexibility. Some provisions can be amended by a simple majority in Parliament, while others require a special majority, and certain amendments need ratification by at least half of the State legislatures.
    • Adaptability: This blend allows the Constitution to be a living document, capable of evolving with changing circumstances while maintaining its core principles.

    3. Federal System with Unitary Bias

    • Division of Powers: India has a federal system of government with a clear division of powers between the Union and the States, outlined in the Seventh Schedule, which includes the Union List, State List, and Concurrent List.
    • Unitary Features: Despite being federal in nature, the Indian Constitution exhibits a unitary bias in times of emergency, where the central government can assume greater control over the states.

    4. Parliamentary Form of Government

    • Democratic Framework: The Indian Constitution adopts a parliamentary system of government, modeled after the British system, where the executive is accountable to the legislature.
    • Collective Responsibility: The Prime Minister and the Council of Ministers are collectively responsible to the Lok Sabha, the lower house of Parliament.

    5. Fundamental Rights

    • Guarantee of Civil Liberties: Part III of the Constitution enumerates Fundamental Rights, which are justiciable and enforceable by the courts.
    • Types of Rights: These include the Right to Equality, Right to Freedom, Right against Exploitation, Right to Freedom of Religion, Cultural and Educational Rights, and the Right to Constitutional Remedies.

    6. Directive Principles of State Policy (DPSPs)

    • Guiding Principles: Part IV of the Constitution contains the Directive Principles of State Policy, which are non-justiciable guidelines for the state to follow in governance.
    • Social Welfare Goals: DPSPs promote ideals such as the provision of adequate livelihood, equal pay for equal work, the right to work, education, and public assistance, and the protection of the environment.

    7. Fundamental Duties

    • Citizen’s Responsibilities: The Fundamental Duties, added by the 42nd Amendment in 1976, are outlined in Part IVA of the Constitution.
    • Examples: Duties include respecting the national symbols, safeguarding public property, promoting harmony, and preserving the rich heritage and environment of the country.

    8. Secular State

    • No State Religion: The Indian Constitution declares India as a secular state, meaning that the state does not favor any religion and provides equal treatment to all religions.
    • Freedom of Conscience: The government does not interfere in religious matters, ensuring freedom of conscience and the right to profess, practice, and propagate any religion.

    9. Independent Judiciary

    • Guardian of the Constitution: The judiciary in India is independent and acts as the guardian and interpreter of the Constitution.
    • Judicial Review: The power of judicial review allows the courts to declare any law or executive action unconstitutional if it violates the provisions of the Constitution.

    10. Single Citizenship

    • National Unity: Unlike many federal countries, India provides for single citizenship. Every citizen, irrespective of the state of their domicile, is recognized as a citizen of India, which promotes national unity and integrity.

    11. Universal Adult Franchise

    • Democratic Participation: The Constitution grants universal adult suffrage, allowing every citizen above the age of 18, regardless of gender, caste, religion, or wealth, to vote in elections.

    12. Emergency Provisions

    • Safeguard Mechanisms: The Constitution includes provisions to deal with emergencies that threaten the security and integrity of the country.
    • Types of Emergencies: These are classified as National Emergency, State Emergency (President's Rule), and Financial Emergency.
    • Centralized Control: During emergencies, the federal structure can be altered, with significant powers shifting to the central government.

    13. Special Provisions for Certain States

    • Asymmetrical Federalism: The Constitution makes special provisions for certain states with unique circumstances, such as Jammu and Kashmir (Article 370, now abrogated), and special provisions for the northeastern states (Article 371).

    14. Provision for an Independent Election Commission

    • Free and Fair Elections: The Constitution establishes an independent Election Commission responsible for the conduct of free and fair elections to Parliament, State Legislatures, and the offices of the President and Vice-President.

    15. Provision for Local Governments

    • Decentralization of Power: The Constitution was amended (73rd and 74th Amendments) to include provisions for Panchayats (rural local governments) and Municipalities (urban local governments), ensuring a more decentralized form of governance.
